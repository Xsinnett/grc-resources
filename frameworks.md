 GRC Frameworks

A breakdown of the most widely used governance, risk, and compliance 
frameworks. Understanding these is essential for any GRC professional.

---

## 1. NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

**Developed by:** National Institute of Standards and Technology  
**Website:** https://www.nist.gov/cyberframework  
**Best for:** Organizations of any size looking for a flexible, 
risk-based approach to cybersecurity

### Overview
The NIST CSF provides a policy framework of computer security guidance 
for how private sector organizations can assess and improve their 
ability to prevent, detect, and respond to cyberattacks.

### The Five Core Functions
| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Identify** | Understand your environment to manage cybersecurity risk |
| **Protect** | Implement safeguards to ensure delivery of critical services |
| **Detect** | Identify the occurrence of a cybersecurity event |
| **Respond** | Take action regarding a detected cybersecurity incident |
| **Recover** | Maintain resilience and restore capabilities after an incident |

### Key Facts
- Voluntary framework — not legally required but widely adopted
- Used by government agencies and private sector organizations
- NIST CSF 2.0 released in 2024 with expanded guidance
- Free to access and implement

## 2. ISO/IEC 27001

**Developed by:** International Organization for Standardization (ISO)  
**Website:** https://www.iso.org/isoiec-27001-information-security.html  
**Best for:** Organizations seeking internationally recognized 
information security certification

### Overview
ISO 27001 is the international standard for information security 
management systems (ISMS). It provides a systematic approach to 
managing sensitive company information and includes people, processes, 
and technology.

### Key Components
- **ISMS** — Information Security Management System
- **Risk Assessment** — Identifying and evaluating information 
  security risks
- **Controls** — 93 controls across 4 themes (Organizational, 
  People, Physical, Technological)
- **Certification** — Organizations can be independently audited 
  and certified

### Key Facts
- Internationally recognized and respected
- Certification demonstrates commitment to security to clients 
  and partners
- Requires ongoing maintenance and annual surveillance audits
- Works well alongside ISO 27701 for privacy management

## 3. SOC 2

**Developed by:** American Institute of CPAs (AICPA)  
**Website:** https://www.aicpa.org/soc2  
**Best for:** Service organizations that store, process, or transmit 
customer data — especially SaaS companies

### Overview
SOC 2 (System and Organization Controls 2) is an auditing procedure 
that ensures service providers securely manage data to protect the 
interests and privacy of their clients.

### The Five Trust Service Criteria
| Criteria | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Security** | Protection against unauthorized access (required) |
| **Availability** | System is available for operation as agreed |
| **Processing Integrity** | System processing is complete and accurate |
| **Confidentiality** | Information designated as confidential is protected |
| **Privacy** | Personal information is collected and used appropriately |

### Type I vs Type II
- **Type I** — Reports on controls at a point in time
- **Type II** — Reports on controls over a period of time 
  (typically 6-12 months) — more trusted by customers

### Key Facts
- Not a certification — it is an audit report
- Commonly required by enterprise customers before signing contracts
- Security criteria is the only mandatory criteria
- Audits must be performed by a licensed CPA firm

## 4. NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)

**Developed by:** National Institute of Standards and Technology  
**Website:** https://csrc.nist.gov/projects/risk-management  
**Best for:** Federal agencies and organizations working with the 
U.S. government

### Overview
The NIST RMF provides a comprehensive, flexible, repeatable, and 
measurable seven-step process that any organization can use to manage 
information security and privacy risk.

### The Seven Steps
1. **Prepare** — Essential activities to prepare the organization
2. **Categorize** — Categorize the system and information processed
3. **Select** — Select the appropriate controls
4. **Implement** — Implement the controls
5. **Assess** — Assess whether controls are implemented correctly
6. **Authorize** — Senior official authorizes system operation
7. **Monitor** — Continuously monitor controls and risks

### Key Facts
- Mandatory for U.S. federal agencies
- Closely tied to NIST SP 800-53 security controls
- Works alongside the NIST CSF
- Essential knowledge for anyone pursuing government GRC roles

## 5. COBIT

**Developed by:** ISACA  
**Website:** https://www.isaca.org/resources/cobit  
**Best for:** Organizations focused on IT governance and management

### Overview
COBIT (Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies) 
is a framework for the governance and management of enterprise 
information and technology. It helps organizations create optimal 
value from IT while maintaining risk at an acceptable level.

### Key Components
- **Governance** — Evaluate, Direct, Monitor (EDM)
- **Management** — Align, Plan, Organize (APO) / Build, Acquire, 
  Implement (BAI) / Deliver, Service, Support (DSS) / Monitor, 
  Evaluate, Assess (MEA)

### Key Facts
- Widely used in large enterprises and financial institutions
- Complements other frameworks like NIST and ISO 27001
- COBIT 2019 is the current version
- ISACA offers COBIT certifications

## 6. PCI DSS

**Developed by:** Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council  
**Website:** https://www.pcisecuritystandards.org  
**Best for:** Any organization that accepts, processes, stores, 
or transmits credit card information

### Overview
The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS) is a 
set of security standards designed to ensure that all companies 
that accept, process, store, or transmit credit card information 
maintain a secure environment.

### The Six Goals
1. Build and maintain a secure network and systems
2. Protect cardholder data
3. Maintain a vulnerability management program
4. Implement strong access control measures
5. Regularly monitor and test networks
6. Maintain an information security policy

### Key Facts
- Mandatory for any organization handling payment card data
- Four levels of compliance based on transaction volume
- Non-compliance can result in fines and loss of ability to 
  process card payments
- PCI DSS v4.0 is the current version
